Form 5472: Foreign-Owned US LLC Reporting

The IRS charges $25,000 per missed filing. Most foreign-owned LLCs don't know they owe it.

Fix This Now →
What It Is

The most expensive form most founders have never heard of

Form 5472 is an annual information return required for any US LLC that has 25% or more foreign ownership — or any LLC that engaged in a reportable transaction with a foreign related party. It is filed as an attachment to the LLC's Form 1120 (or 1065 for multi-member entities) and is due on the same schedule as the underlying return.

This is a pure information return. It does not create a tax liability. The IRS uses it to track money flows between foreign persons and US entities. But the penalty for failure to file bears no relationship to the amount at stake: $25,000 per form, per year, with a second $25,000 assessment if the failure continues after IRS notice.

Critically, there is no de minimis exception and no revenue threshold. An LLC with $0 in revenue that received a capital contribution from a foreign founder is still required to file. Single-member LLCs that are disregarded entities for tax purposes are explicitly included in the requirement.

Who This Affects

Three situations that trigger Form 5472

Scenario 01

Foreign national founding a US LLC

You're based outside the US and incorporated a Delaware or Wyoming LLC to operate your SaaS or e-commerce business. Even as a single-member disregarded entity, you are a foreign person owning 100% of a US LLC — Form 5472 is required every year.

Scenario 02

US founder with foreign co-founders at 25%+

You founded a company in the US but brought on co-founders abroad who collectively own 30% of the LLC. That crosses the 25% foreign ownership threshold. The LLC must file Form 5472 regardless of how small the company is or how little money has moved.

Scenario 03

LLC receiving capital from a foreign parent

Your US subsidiary received a capital contribution, loan, or intercompany payment from a foreign parent or related entity. That is a reportable transaction under the Form 5472 rules — filing is required even if foreign ownership is below 25%.

The Risk

A penalty with no proportionality

$25,000
Per missed or late filing — per year

The IRS assesses the $25,000 penalty automatically for failure to file or for filing an incomplete or inaccurate Form 5472. There is no warning system. There is no first-offense leniency built into the statute for this particular form.

If you receive an IRS notice after the missed filing and fail to respond with a corrected return within 90 days, a second $25,000 penalty is assessed. A company that missed filings for three years without knowing they were required could face $75,000 in penalties before the underlying tax issue — often zero — is ever addressed.

Reasonable cause relief is technically available but difficult to obtain. The IRS takes the position that taxpayers are responsible for knowing their filing requirements. A CPA who was unaware of Form 5472 is generally not considered reasonable cause.
