  - title: "Schedule C Is Often Simpler, But It Is Not Casual"
    body: |
      Schedule C is the tax home for sole proprietors and single-member LLCs that have not elected corporate treatment. It is often the cleanest starting point for a business because there is no separate federal entity return, but that does not mean the tax work is simple. Income reporting, deductible expenses, self-employment tax, bookkeeping quality, and owner recordkeeping all matter.

      For many businesses, Schedule C is exactly the right fit. The question is not whether it looks less formal than a corporation. The question is whether it matches the size, profit level, and operating reality of the business.
  - title: "What Usually Shows Up on a Schedule C Return"
    body: |
      Common issues on Schedule C returns include:

      * Business income reporting from one or more 1099s or direct customer payments
      * Expense categorization and cleanup when the books are incomplete
      * Home office, vehicle, supplies, software, and contractor deduction analysis
      * Self-employment tax and quarterly estimated tax planning
      * Coordination between the business activity and the owner's Form 1040

      The return may look like one schedule, but the planning work behind it affects both income tax and self-employment tax.
  - title: "When Schedule C Is the Right Fit"
    body: |
      Schedule C often works well for solo operators, early-stage businesses, consultants, online sellers, and service businesses that are still proving out the economics. It can also be the right long-term structure when the business is profitable but not yet at the point where payroll, a corporate return, and the compliance burden of another entity create a real net benefit.

      We do not treat entity changes as a default upgrade. Sometimes staying on Schedule C is the better answer.
  - title: "When It May Be Time to Revisit the Structure"
    body: |
      Once profit grows, the question usually shifts from "How do I file this?" to "Is this still the best structure?" That is where S-corp analysis often enters the conversation. In other cases, bringing on another owner or changing the economics points more toward partnership treatment instead.

      We review that question with actual numbers, not generic advice. The right answer depends on profit, payroll readiness, owner plans, and how the business is likely to evolve.
