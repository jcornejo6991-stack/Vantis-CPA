  - title: "Tax Preparation and Planning for Individuals and Families"
    body: |
      Most individuals assume their tax situation is straightforward until the year it is not. A job change, a stock sale, a rental property, an RSU vest, or a K-1 from a partnership can turn a routine return into something that requires real decisions. By the time those decisions reach a preparer, some of the planning opportunities have already closed. We work with individuals and families whose income comes from multiple sources and who want a CPA involved before the transactions happen, not just when it is time to report them.
  - title: "What Individuals and Families Need"
    body: |
      Common needs include:

      * W-2 income, including multiple employers and mid-year job changes
      * 1099-NEC and self-employment income (Schedule C)
      * K-1 income from partnerships, S-corporations, or trusts
      * Rental property income and expenses (Schedule E)
      * Investment income, dividends, and capital gains
      * RSUs, stock options (NSO and ISO), and equity compensation
      * Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS / Section 1202)
      * Active traders, wash sale rules, and mark-to-market elections
      * Estimated quarterly tax planning
      * Sale of a primary residence or investment property
      * IRS and state notice response
      * Amended and prior year returns
      * Multi-state returns where applicable
      * Year-end tax planning
  - title: "The Decisions That Actually Reduce Tax"
    body: |
      The tax code has real provisions that benefit individuals who use them: retirement contributions that reduce taxable income before year-end, tax-loss harvesting that offsets gains across a portfolio, basis planning on assets held for sale, and timing of income and deductions across years. Capital gains rates reward holding periods. Equity compensation has planning windows that close at exercise or vesting. Estimated payments that are calibrated to actual income avoid penalties and eliminate the surprise of a large balance due in April.

      None of this happens automatically. It requires someone watching the full picture throughout the year and flagging decisions before the window closes, not reconstructing what happened after the fact.
  - title: "Life Events That Change the Calculation"
    body: |
      Certain events reshape the return in ways most filers are not prepared for. Changing jobs mid-year affects withholding and may trigger estimated tax requirements. Receiving RSUs that vest creates ordinary income that does not always match what the broker reports. Selling a home triggers gain calculations that depend on ownership period, use, and improvements. Getting married or divorced changes filing status, income thresholds, and deduction strategies. Receiving an inheritance may involve a step-up in basis or reporting requirements for foreign assets. Starting a rental property opens up depreciation, passive activity rules, and Schedule E reporting.

      These situations rarely fix themselves on a standard return and often require planning before the event, not just reporting after.
  - title: "When Something Goes Wrong"
    body: |
      IRS and state tax notices, prior year errors, and missed filings are stressful to handle alone. A notice does not always mean you owe more. Many are automated responses to mismatched information that can be resolved with a clear written reply. Prior year returns can be amended when errors are discovered, and in some cases refunds can be recovered going back several years. If you have fallen behind on filing, there are structured ways to come back into compliance without penalties compounding further. We handle the response and the resolution, not just the current year return.
